Intraday Return and Volatility Modeling of Futures Contracts

Introduction¶

This notebook models intraday dynamics for 9 CME futures contracts across U.S. Treasuries (ZT 2Y, ZF 5Y, TN 10Y, ZN 10Y, ZB 30Y, UB Ultra Bond), energy (BZ Brent Crude, CL WTI Crude), and FX (6E Euro). We fit two classical time-series models to 10-second resampled log returns — ARIMA for the mean of returns and GARCH for volatility — and see how well they actually work out-of-sample.

We start with ARIMA. Its residual diagnostics (heteroskedasticity, heavy tails, remaining autocorrelation) motivate why we then move to GARCH. Both models are evaluated on a chronological 70/15/15 train/validation/test split.

The data is MBO (market-by-order) trade data stored as parquet in S3, processed through an AWS EMR Spark cluster because the raw dataset is huge (~3M+ rows per ticker). The sample runs from Nov 18, 2025 to Jan 20, 2026.

Background¶

Why Log Returns¶

We work with log returns instead of prices because prices are non-stationary — they trend and drift, which breaks the assumptions of most time-series models. Log returns (rₜ = log(Pₜ) − log(Pₜ₋₁)) fluctuate around a stable mean near zero, are additive over time, and approximate percentage changes for small values. We assume therefore log returns are stationary.

Volatility Clustering¶

One of the most consistent patterns in financial data is that large moves tend to follow large moves, and quiet periods tend to stay quiet. This is volatility clustering. Even if returns themselves are uncorrelated, their squares are — meaning variance isn’t constant, it evolves over time. This is called conditional heteroskedasticity, and it’s what GARCH is designed to capture. It’s also what makes ARIMA insufficient on its own.

Heavy Tails¶

Financial returns have much heavier events in tails than a normal distribution: extreme moves happen way more often than a bell curve predicts. This shows up as high kurtosis (a Gaussian has kurtosis of 3; we see values from 60 to 5,800 in this data). To deal with this, the GARCH model uses a Student-t distribution for innovations, where the degrees-of-freedom parameter ν controls how heavy the tails are. Lower ν = heavier tails.

Modeling Approach¶

ARIMA Model¶

ARIMA(1,0,1) models whether past returns help predict future returns (chosen via looking at lags in ACF/PACF plots)

(1)

We use ACF, PACF, Ljung-Box tests, and residual diagnostics to check whether it’s capturing the structure in the data.

GARCH Model¶

GARCH(1,1) lets the variance change over time based on recent shocks and recent variance:

σt2=ω+α1εt−12+β1σt−12\sigma_t^2 = \omega + \alpha_1 \varepsilon_{t-1}^2 + \beta_1 \sigma_{t-1}^2σt2​=ω+α1​εt−12​+β1​σt−12​
(2)

α₁ controls how much a big return spikes next-period variance, β₁ controls how slowly that spike fades. When α₁ + β₁ is close to 1, volatility shocks are very persistent. We use a zero-mean specification (expected return = 0) since ARIMA already showed the constant is insignificant everywhere.

Pipeline¶

For each ticker:

  1. Extract trade data from S3/Spark for the chosen date range

  2. Resample irregular ticks to a 10-second grid using last-price with forward fill

  3. Compute log returns within each session (so overnight gaps don’t contaminate the series)

  4. Split chronologically into 70/15/15 train/val/test

  5. ARIMA — fit on training, check residual diagnostics, forecast on val and test

  6. GARCH — fit on training, check standardized residual diagnostics, produce one-step-ahead volatility forecasts, compare against a constant-volatility baseline using MAE, RMSE, and QLIKE

Significance¶

Though ARIMA shows insignifcant, and essentially no drift, this suggests that trading directionally is difficult to find alpha in. However, predictive power increases in GARCH: allowing for volatility controlled strategies.

Some potential uses in real life for practioners (one trading strategy at bottom of notebook as well)¶

Some potential use cases could be:

  1. Risk Management - most asset managers are trying to have a certain controlled level of volatility in their portfolios. If they can predict a certain level of volatility in the future, they can decide to allocate to risky assets differently.

  2. Trading Vol - traders or hedge funds can use this strategy to trade volatility, either via options or volatility future.

  3. Timing of hedging activity - if traders want to own hedges or want to put on trades, choosing a time before high volatility would get better execution and potential payout if they were to buy options.

Data Description¶

This dataset contains intraday market data for selected ticker of futures contracts over a chosen date range from nov 2025 to jan 2026. Each record represents a time bucket within a trading session and includes both the observed filled price and the corresponding log return, when available.

Variables¶

  • symbol: the current future ticker

  • session_date: the trading date

  • second_bucket: intraday timestamp for each observation

  • filled_price: filled in price at time

  • log_return: log return of the future

Processing steps¶

The data is:

  1. Filtered to a single ticker and date range

  2. Sorted by session_date and second_bucket

  3. Downsampled to 10-second intervals

  4. Split into two series:

    • price observations where filled_price is not null

    • return observations where log_return is not null

Visualizations - see graphs for prices, log returns for each future¶

Price plot¶

The first chart shows a time series of the price over time for each trading session.
Each line and color represents one session_date, making it easy to compare intraday price behavior across days.

Log return plot¶

The second chart shows log_return over time for each trading session.
This highlights short-term price changes and makes intraday volatility patterns easier to see.

Summary¶

Together, these plots provide a view of:

  • how the ticker price evolves during the trading day

  • how returns fluctuate at a high frequency

  • how intraday behavior differs across sessions

ARIMA Modeling¶

We use ARIMA as a baseline model for the mean behavior of intraday log returns. The goal is not to fully model volatility, but to check whether there is any short-run structure in the returns that can be captured with a simple time-series model.

For each ticker, the return series is split in chronological order into training, validation, and test sets. An ARIMA(1,0,1) model is fitted on the training data, then used to generate forecasts on the validation set. We chose this because looking at the lags, usually 1 is significant in autocorrelation.

This setup lets us measure how well a basic linear model explains the return dynamics before moving to volatility-specific models. In most cases, ARIMA captures only limited dependence in returns, while the residuals still show changing variance and heavy tails. That is why ARIMA is treated here as a benchmark for the conditional mean, not as the final model for the full series behavior.

from pyspark.sql import functions as F
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA
from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox

def run_arima_one_ticker(
    mbo_spark_df,
    ticker="CL.c.0",
    start_date="2025-11-18",
    end_date="2026-01-20",
    freq="10s",
    order=(1, 0, 1),
    single_session_only=None,
    min_points_per_session=30
):
    df = (
        mbo_spark_df
        .filter(
            (F.col("symbol") == ticker) &
            (F.col("session_date") >= F.lit(start_date)) &
            (F.col("session_date") <= F.lit(end_date))
        )
        .select("session_date", "second_bucket", "filled_price")
        .where(F.col("second_bucket").isNotNull())
        .orderBy("session_date", "second_bucket")
    )

    if single_session_only is not None:
        df = df.filter(F.col("session_date") == F.lit(single_session_only))

    pdf = df.toPandas()

    if pdf.empty:
        raise ValueError(f"No data for {ticker} between {start_date} and {end_date}")

    pdf["session_date"] = pd.to_datetime(pdf["session_date"])
    pdf["second_bucket"] = pd.to_datetime(pdf["second_bucket"])
    pdf = pdf.sort_values(["session_date", "second_bucket"]).reset_index(drop=True)

    print(f"{ticker} | rows: {len(pdf)}")
    print("Sessions:", sorted(pdf["session_date"].dt.strftime("%Y-%m-%d").unique()))

    # Resample per session
    session_frames = []

    for session, g in pdf.groupby("session_date"):
        g = g.sort_values("second_bucket").dropna(subset=["filled_price"])

        if g.empty:
            continue

        g = g.set_index("second_bucket")

        s = g["filled_price"].resample(freq).last()
        s = s.ffill().dropna()

        if len(s) < min_points_per_session:
            continue

        tmp = s.to_frame("filled_price").reset_index()
        tmp["session_date"] = pd.to_datetime(session)
        session_frames.append(tmp)

    if not session_frames:
        raise ValueError("No sessions")

    ts_df = pd.concat(session_frames, ignore_index=True)
    ts_df = ts_df.sort_values(["session_date", "second_bucket"]).reset_index(drop=True)


    ts_df["log_price"] = np.log(ts_df["filled_price"])
    ts_df["log_return"] = ts_df.groupby("session_date")["log_price"].diff()

    model_df = ts_df.dropna(subset=["log_return"]).copy()

    series = model_df.set_index("second_bucket")["log_return"]
    series = series[~series.index.duplicated()]

    if len(series) < 100:
        raise ValueError("No data")

    print(f"Resampled points: {len(series)}")

    # Train / val / test split 70/15/15
    n = len(series)
    train_end = int(n * 0.70)
    val_end = int(n * 0.85)

    train = series.iloc[:train_end]
    val = series.iloc[train_end:val_end]
    test = series.iloc[val_end:]

    print(f"Train: {len(train)} | Val: {len(val)} | Test: {len(test)}")

    # Plots
    plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
    plt.plot(series, linewidth=1)
    plt.title(f"{ticker} log return")
    plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
    plt.show()

    plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
    plt.plot(train, linewidth=1)
    plt.title("Training log return")
    plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
    plt.show()

    plot_acf(train.dropna(), lags=50)
    plt.show()

    plot_pacf(train.dropna(), lags=50, method="ywm")
    plt.show()

    # Fit on training set
    model_train = ARIMA(train, order=order)
    results_train = model_train.fit()

    print(results_train.summary())

    # residuals
    resid = results_train.resid.dropna()

    plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
    plt.plot(resid, linewidth=1)
    plt.title("Residuals (train)")
    plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
    plt.show()

    plot_acf(resid, lags=50)
    plt.show()

    lb = acorr_ljungbox(resid, lags=[10, 20, 30], return_df=True)
    print("\nLjung-Box:\n", lb)

    # Validation forecast
    val_forecast = results_train.get_forecast(steps=len(val))
    val_pred = val_forecast.predicted_mean
    val_ci = val_forecast.conf_int()

    val_pred.index = val.index
    val_ci.index = val.index

    val_mae = np.mean(np.abs(val - val_pred))
    val_rmse = np.sqrt(np.mean((val - val_pred) ** 2))

    plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))
    plt.plot(train, label="Train")
    plt.plot(val, label="Val actual")
    plt.plot(val_pred, label="Val pred")

    plt.fill_between(
        val_ci.index,
        val_ci.iloc[:, 0],
        val_ci.iloc[:, 1],
        alpha=0.2
    )

    plt.legend()
    plt.title("Validation forecast: log returns")
    plt.grid(alpha=0.3)
    plt.show()

    print(f"\nVal MAE: {val_mae:.6f} | RMSE: {val_rmse:.6f}")

    # Refit on training + validation
    train_val = series.iloc[:val_end]
    results_train_val = ARIMA(train_val, order=order).fit()

    # Test forecast
    test_forecast = results_train_val.get_forecast(steps=len(test))
    test_pred = test_forecast.predicted_mean
    test_ci = test_forecast.conf_int()

    test_pred.index = test.index
    test_ci.index = test.index

    test_mae = np.mean(np.abs(test - test_pred))

ARIMA summary below¶

We fit the same ARIMA(1,0,1) model to every ticker to keep the mean-model comparison consistent across assets. A few patterns show up pretty clearly.

First, the constant is not significant for any of the tickers, which is what we would expect at a 10-second frequency since the average log return is basically zero. The AR(1) and MA(1) terms are usually significant, but in a lot of cases they offset each other quite a bit. For example, TN has AR = 0.38 and MA = -0.50, while BZ has AR = -0.54 and MA = 0.53. That kind of near-cancellation looks more like microstructure effects, like bid-ask bounce, than real predictability.

The residual diagnostics are still pretty bad. The Ljung-Box results at lags 10, 20, and 30 are significant for most of the tickers, so there is still serial dependence left in the residuals. TN, ZF, and ZN are especially clear examples. BZ and CL look a little better than the others, but overall the ARIMA model is not fully cleaning up the dependence in the series.

The residuals are also very far from normal. Kurtosis is huge across all tickers, from about 62 for CL up to more than 5,800 for ZT. That is not surprising for high-frequency data, but it does show that a constant-variance Gaussian-type setup is a bad fit here. For ZT in particular, the extreme kurtosis probably comes from a lot of zero returns mixed with occasional jumps.

We also see strong evidence of heteroskedasticity. The Prob(H) values are essentially 0 for every ticker, which means the variance is changing over time and ARIMA is not built to handle that.

There are also some numerical issues in the fits. Every model gave a maximum likelihood convergence warning, and several of the covariance matrices are singular or close to singular. So while the coefficient estimates themselves may still be usable as rough signals, we should not put too much weight on the reported standard errors or z-statistics.

In terms of forecasting, the validation and test MAE/RMSE values are all very small in absolute size, but that is mostly because 10-second returns are themselves extremely close to zero. In practice, the forecasts flatten out quickly and mostly predict something close to zero. So ARIMA does not add much for multi-step forecasting at this frequency.

A few ticker-specific differences also stand out. Energy contracts like CL and BZ have the largest forecast errors, which matches their higher volatility. Shorter-duration Treasuries like ZT and ZF have the smallest errors, which fits their lower per-tick volatility. 6E also has an extremely small MAE, but that mostly reflects how often the return is near zero rather than strong model performance.

Overall, ARIMA(1,0,1) works better here as a simple benchmark for the conditional mean than as a full model for the series. The remaining autocorrelation, heavy tails, and changing variance all point toward volatility models as the more important next step.

GARCH analysis¶

This part of the project uses a GARCH model to study intraday log returns for each future. GARCH makes sense here because financial returns often show volatility clustering, where big moves tend to be followed by big moves and quiet periods tend to stay quiet.

First, the raw trade data is filtered to one ticker and a chosen date range. The prices are then resampled to a fixed time interval so the data is on a regular grid. Returns are computed within each trading session, which is important because it avoids creating fake overnight returns between sessions.

A few important modeling choices:

The data is split in time order into training, validation, and test sets, which makes the forecasting setup more realistic. The code also allows different volatility models.

We fit a standard GARCH(1,1) model with Student-t innovations to model volatility clustering and heavy-tailed return behavior.

To check whether GARCH is appropriate, we look at the ACF of returns and the ACF of squared returns. Squared returns are especially important because autocorrelation there suggests volatility clustering, which is exactly what GARCH is designed to capture. After fitting the model, we check standardized residuals, their ACF, the ACF of squared standardized residuals, and Ljung–Box tests to see whether the model has removed most of the remaining structure.

For forecasting, we used rolling one-step-ahead forecasts. The model is fit on the training data, evaluated on validation, then refit on train plus validation and tested on the final test set. Forecasted volatility is compared to a realized-volatility proxy based on a rolling standard deviation of returns, and also to a simple constant-volatility baseline.

Performance is measured using MAE, RMSE, MSE, and QLIKE. Overall, this setup is meant to test whether a GARCH-style model can capture time-varying volatility better than a simple baseline, while keeping the evaluation realistic and time ordered.

from pyspark.sql import functions as F
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf
from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
from arch import arch_model


def run_garch_one_ticker_log_returns_only(
    mbo_spark_df,
    ticker="CL.c.0",
    start_date="2025-11-18",
    end_date="2026-01-20",
    freq="10s",
    single_session_only=None,
    min_points_per_session=30,
    train_frac=0.70,
    val_frac=0.15,
    mean_model="Zero",
    vol_model="GARCH",
    p=1,
    q=1,
    o=0,
    dist="normal",
    scale_returns=100.0,
    realized_vol_window=6,
    trading_days_per_year=252,
    trading_seconds_per_day=23 * 60 * 60,
    show_plots=True
):

    # data extraction
    df = (
        mbo_spark_df
        .filter(
            (F.col("symbol") == ticker) &
            (F.col("session_date") >= F.lit(start_date)) &
            (F.col("session_date") <= F.lit(end_date))
        )
        .select("session_date", "second_bucket", "filled_price")
        .where(F.col("second_bucket").isNotNull())
        .orderBy("session_date", "second_bucket")
    )

    if single_session_only is not None:
        df = df.filter(F.col("session_date") == F.lit(single_session_only))

    pdf = df.toPandas()

    if pdf.empty:
        raise ValueError(f"No data for {ticker} from {start_date} to {end_date}")

    pdf["session_date"] = pd.to_datetime(pdf["session_date"])
    pdf["second_bucket"] = pd.to_datetime(pdf["second_bucket"])
    pdf = pdf.sort_values(["session_date", "second_bucket"]).reset_index(drop=True)

    print(f"Ticker: {ticker}")
    print(f"Raw rows: {len(pdf)}")
    print(f"Sessions: {sorted(pdf['session_date'].dt.strftime('%Y-%m-%d').unique().tolist())}")

    # resample each session
    session_frames = []

    for session, g in pdf.groupby("session_date"):
        g = g.sort_values("second_bucket").copy()
        g = g.dropna(subset=["filled_price"])

        if g.empty:
            continue

        g = g.set_index("second_bucket")
        s = g["filled_price"].resample(freq).last().ffill().dropna()

        if len(s) < min_points_per_session:
            continue

        tmp = s.to_frame(name="filled_price").reset_index()
        tmp["session_date"] = pd.to_datetime(session)
        session_frames.append(tmp)

    if not session_frames:
        raise ValueError("No usable sessions after resampling.")

    ts_df = pd.concat(session_frames, ignore_index=True)
    ts_df = ts_df.sort_values(["session_date", "second_bucket"]).reset_index(drop=True)

    # build log returns
    ts_df["log_price"] = np.log(ts_df["filled_price"])
    ts_df["log_return"] = ts_df.groupby("session_date")["log_price"].diff()
    ts_df = ts_df.dropna(subset=["log_return"]).copy()

    series_return = ts_df.set_index("second_bucket")["log_return"].copy()
    series_session = ts_df.set_index("second_bucket")["session_date"].copy()

    keep_mask = ~series_return.index.duplicated(keep="first")
    series_return = series_return[keep_mask]
    series_session = series_session[keep_mask]

    if len(series_return) < 200:
        raise ValueError("Too few observations")

    print(f"Usable return observations: {len(series_return)}")

    # split train, validation, and test
    n = len(series_return)
    train_end = int(n * train_frac)
    val_end = int(n * (train_frac + val_frac))

    train_ret = series_return.iloc[:train_end]
    val_ret = series_return.iloc[train_end:val_end]
    test_ret = series_return.iloc[val_end:]

    print(f"Train: {len(train_ret)} | Val: {len(val_ret)} | Test: {len(test_ret)}")

    if len(train_ret) < 100 or len(val_ret) < 20 or len(test_ret) < 20:
        raise ValueError("One of the splits is too small.")

    # annualization
    freq_seconds = pd.to_timedelta(freq).total_seconds()
    periods_per_day = trading_seconds_per_day / freq_seconds
    annualization_factor = np.sqrt(periods_per_day * trading_days_per_year)

    print(f"Freq: {freq_seconds}s | Periods/day: {periods_per_day} | Ann. factor: {annualization_factor:.2f}")

    # exploratory plots
    if show_plots:
        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
        plt.plot(series_return.index, series_return.values, linewidth=1)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} log returns ({freq})")
        plt.xlabel("Time")
        plt.ylabel("Log return")
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
        plt.plot(train_ret.index, train_ret.values, linewidth=1)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} train log returns")
        plt.xlabel("Time")
        plt.ylabel("Log return")
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plot_acf(train_ret.dropna(), lags=50)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} ACF of log returns")
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plot_acf(train_ret.dropna() ** 2, lags=50)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} ACF of squared log returns")
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

    # choose volatility model
    all_scaled = series_return * scale_returns

    vol_key = vol_model.upper().replace("-", "")
    if vol_key == "GARCH":
        arch_kwargs = dict(vol="GARCH", p=p, q=q, o=0)
    else:
        raise ValueError("vol_model not valid")

    model = arch_model(
        all_scaled,
        mean=mean_model,
        dist=dist,
        rescale=False,
        **arch_kwargs
    )

    # fit model on train
    results_train = model.fit(last_obs=train_ret.index[-1], disp="off")

    print("\nTrain model summary")
    print(results_train.summary())

    # residual diagnostics
    resid = pd.Series(results_train.resid, index=series_return.index).loc[train_ret.index].dropna()
    cond_vol_train_scaled = pd.Series(
        results_train.conditional_volatility,
        index=series_return.index
    ).loc[train_ret.index].dropna()

    std_resid = (resid / cond_vol_train_scaled).replace([np.inf, -np.inf], np.nan).dropna()

    lb_resid = acorr_ljungbox(std_resid, lags=[10, 20, 30], return_df=True)
    lb_sq = acorr_ljungbox(std_resid ** 2, lags=[10, 20, 30], return_df=True)

    print("\nLjung-Box on standardized residuals:")
    print(lb_resid)

    print("\nLjung-Box on squared standardized residuals:")
    print(lb_sq)

    if show_plots:
        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
        plt.plot(std_resid.index, std_resid.values, linewidth=1)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} standardized residuals (train)")
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plot_acf(std_resid, lags=50)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} ACF of standardized residuals")
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plot_acf(std_resid ** 2, lags=50)
        plt.title(f"{ticker} ACF of squared standardized residuals")
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
        plt.plot(
            cond_vol_train_scaled.index,
            cond_vol_train_scaled.values / scale_returns,
            linewidth=1
        )
        plt.title(f"{ticker} conditional volatility (train, raw)")
        plt.ylabel("Raw volatility")
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 4))
        plt.plot(
            cond_vol_train_scaled.index,
            (cond_vol_train_scaled.values / scale_returns) * annualization_factor,
            linewidth=1
        )
        plt.title(f"{ticker} conditional volatility (train, annualized)")
        plt.ylabel("Annualized volatility")
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

    # validation forecast
    val_forecast_res = results_train.forecast(start=val_ret.index[0], horizon=1, reindex=True)

    val_var_all = val_forecast_res.variance.dropna()
    val_var_scaled = val_var_all.loc[val_ret.index, "h.1"]
    val_vol_forecast = np.sqrt(val_var_scaled) / scale_returns

    val_realized_vol = val_ret.rolling(realized_vol_window).std()
    val_realized_vol_ann = val_realized_vol * annualization_factor
    val_vol_forecast_ann = val_vol_forecast * annualization_factor

    train_vol_const = train_ret.std()
    val_baseline_vol = pd.Series(train_vol_const, index=val_ret.index)
    val_baseline_vol_ann = val_baseline_vol * annualization_factor

    val_eval = pd.concat([
        val_ret.rename("ret"),
        val_realized_vol.rename("realized_vol"),
        val_realized_vol_ann.rename("realized_vol_ann"),
        val_vol_forecast.rename("forecast_vol"),
        val_vol_forecast_ann.rename("forecast_vol_ann"),
        val_baseline_vol.rename("baseline_vol"),
        val_baseline_vol_ann.rename("baseline_vol_ann"),
    ], axis=1).dropna()

    if val_eval.empty:
        raise ValueError("Validation  is empty ")

    eps = 1e-12

    # validation metrics
    val_mae_vol = np.mean(np.abs(val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["forecast_vol"]))
    val_rmse_vol = np.sqrt(np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["forecast_vol"]) ** 2))
    val_mse_vol = np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["forecast_vol"]) ** 2)

    val_baseline_mae_vol = np.mean(np.abs(val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["baseline_vol"]))
    val_baseline_rmse_vol = np.sqrt(np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["baseline_vol"]) ** 2))
    val_baseline_mse_vol = np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol"] - val_eval["baseline_vol"]) ** 2)

    val_realized_var = np.maximum(val_eval["realized_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_forecast_var = np.maximum(val_eval["forecast_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_baseline_var = np.maximum(val_eval["baseline_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_qlike = np.mean(np.log(val_forecast_var) + val_realized_var / val_forecast_var)
    val_baseline_qlike = np.mean(np.log(val_baseline_var) + val_realized_var / val_baseline_var)

    val_mae_vol_ann = np.mean(np.abs(val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]))
    val_rmse_vol_ann = np.sqrt(np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]) ** 2))
    val_mse_vol_ann = np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]) ** 2)

    val_baseline_mae_vol_ann = np.mean(np.abs(val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]))
    val_baseline_rmse_vol_ann = np.sqrt(np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]) ** 2))
    val_baseline_mse_vol_ann = np.mean((val_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - val_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]) ** 2)

    val_realized_var_ann = np.maximum(val_eval["realized_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_forecast_var_ann = np.maximum(val_eval["forecast_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_baseline_var_ann = np.maximum(val_eval["baseline_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    val_qlike_ann = np.mean(np.log(val_forecast_var_ann) + val_realized_var_ann / val_forecast_var_ann)
    val_baseline_qlike_ann = np.mean(np.log(val_baseline_var_ann) + val_realized_var_ann / val_baseline_var_ann)

    print("\nVALIDATION METRICS (raw)")
    print(f"  GARCH    — MAE: {val_mae_vol:.10f}  RMSE: {val_rmse_vol:.10f}  QLIKE: {val_qlike:.10f}")
    print(f"  Baseline — MAE: {val_baseline_mae_vol:.10f}  RMSE: {val_baseline_rmse_vol:.10f}  QLIKE: {val_baseline_qlike:.10f}")
    print("VALIDATION METRICS (annualized)")
    print(f"  GARCH    — MAE: {val_mae_vol_ann:.10f}  RMSE: {val_rmse_vol_ann:.10f}  QLIKE: {val_qlike_ann:.10f}")
    print(f"  Baseline — MAE: {val_baseline_mae_vol_ann:.10f}  RMSE: {val_baseline_rmse_vol_ann:.10f}  QLIKE: {val_baseline_qlike_ann:.10f}")

    # validation plots
    if show_plots:
        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))
        plt.plot(train_ret.index, train_ret.rolling(realized_vol_window).std(), label="Train realized vol", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["realized_vol"], label="Val realized vol", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["forecast_vol"], label="Val GARCH forecast", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["baseline_vol"], label="Val baseline (const)", linewidth=1, linestyle="--")
        plt.title(f"{ticker} validation volatility forecast (raw)")
        plt.ylabel("Raw volatility")
        plt.legend()
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["realized_vol_ann"], label="Val realized vol (ann)", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["forecast_vol_ann"], label="Val GARCH forecast (ann)", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(val_eval.index, val_eval["baseline_vol_ann"], label="Val baseline (ann)", linewidth=1, linestyle="--")
        plt.title(f"{ticker} validation volatility forecast (annualized)")
        plt.ylabel("Annualized volatility")
        plt.legend()
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

    # fit model on train and validation
    results_train_val = model.fit(last_obs=val_ret.index[-1], disp="off")

    print("\nTrain+validation summary")
    print(results_train_val.summary())

    # test forecast
    test_forecast_res = results_train_val.forecast(start=test_ret.index[0], horizon=1, reindex=True)

    test_var_all = test_forecast_res.variance.dropna()
    test_var_scaled = test_var_all.loc[test_ret.index, "h.1"]
    test_vol_forecast = np.sqrt(test_var_scaled) / scale_returns

    test_realized_vol = test_ret.rolling(realized_vol_window).std()
    test_realized_vol_ann = test_realized_vol * annualization_factor
    test_vol_forecast_ann = test_vol_forecast * annualization_factor

    train_val_ret = series_return.iloc[:val_end]
    train_val_vol_const = train_val_ret.std()
    test_baseline_vol = pd.Series(train_val_vol_const, index=test_ret.index)
    test_baseline_vol_ann = test_baseline_vol * annualization_factor

    test_eval = pd.concat([
        test_ret.rename("ret"),
        test_realized_vol.rename("realized_vol"),
        test_realized_vol_ann.rename("realized_vol_ann"),
        test_vol_forecast.rename("forecast_vol"),
        test_vol_forecast_ann.rename("forecast_vol_ann"),
        test_baseline_vol.rename("baseline_vol"),
        test_baseline_vol_ann.rename("baseline_vol_ann"),
    ], axis=1).dropna()

    if test_eval.empty:
        raise ValueError("Test eval is empty — realized_vol_window may be too large.")

    # test metrics
    test_mae_vol = np.mean(np.abs(test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["forecast_vol"]))
    test_rmse_vol = np.sqrt(np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["forecast_vol"]) ** 2))
    test_mse_vol = np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["forecast_vol"]) ** 2)

    test_baseline_mae_vol = np.mean(np.abs(test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["baseline_vol"]))
    test_baseline_rmse_vol = np.sqrt(np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["baseline_vol"]) ** 2))
    test_baseline_mse_vol = np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol"] - test_eval["baseline_vol"]) ** 2)

    test_realized_var = np.maximum(test_eval["realized_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_forecast_var = np.maximum(test_eval["forecast_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_baseline_var = np.maximum(test_eval["baseline_vol"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_qlike = np.mean(np.log(test_forecast_var) + test_realized_var / test_forecast_var)
    test_baseline_qlike = np.mean(np.log(test_baseline_var) + test_realized_var / test_baseline_var)

    test_mae_vol_ann = np.mean(np.abs(test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]))
    test_rmse_vol_ann = np.sqrt(np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]) ** 2))
    test_mse_vol_ann = np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["forecast_vol_ann"]) ** 2)

    test_baseline_mae_vol_ann = np.mean(np.abs(test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]))
    test_baseline_rmse_vol_ann = np.sqrt(np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]) ** 2))
    test_baseline_mse_vol_ann = np.mean((test_eval["realized_vol_ann"] - test_eval["baseline_vol_ann"]) ** 2)

    test_realized_var_ann = np.maximum(test_eval["realized_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_forecast_var_ann = np.maximum(test_eval["forecast_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_baseline_var_ann = np.maximum(test_eval["baseline_vol_ann"].values ** 2, eps)
    test_qlike_ann = np.mean(np.log(test_forecast_var_ann) + test_realized_var_ann / test_forecast_var_ann)
    test_baseline_qlike_ann = np.mean(np.log(test_baseline_var_ann) + test_realized_var_ann / test_baseline_var_ann)

    print("\nTEST METRICS (raw)")
    print(f"  GARCH    — MAE: {test_mae_vol:.10f}  RMSE: {test_rmse_vol:.10f}  QLIKE: {test_qlike:.10f}")
    print(f"  Baseline — MAE: {test_baseline_mae_vol:.10f}  RMSE: {test_baseline_rmse_vol:.10f}  QLIKE: {test_baseline_qlike:.10f}")
    print("TEST METRICS (annualized)")
    print(f"  GARCH    — MAE: {test_mae_vol_ann:.10f}  RMSE: {test_rmse_vol_ann:.10f}  QLIKE: {test_qlike_ann:.10f}")
    print(f"  Baseline — MAE: {test_baseline_mae_vol_ann:.10f}  RMSE: {test_baseline_rmse_vol_ann:.10f}  QLIKE: {test_baseline_qlike_ann:.10f}")

    # test plots
    if show_plots:
        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))
        plt.plot(train_val_ret.index, train_val_ret.rolling(realized_vol_window).std(), label="Train+Val realized vol", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["realized_vol"], label="Test realized vol", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["forecast_vol"], label="Test GARCH forecast", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["baseline_vol"], label="Test baseline (const)", linewidth=1, linestyle="--")
        plt.title(f"{ticker} test volatility forecast (raw)")
        plt.ylabel("Raw volatility")
        plt.legend()
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

        plt.figure(figsize=(14, 5))
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["realized_vol_ann"], label="Test realized vol (ann)", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["forecast_vol_ann"], label="Test GARCH forecast (ann)", linewidth=1)
        plt.plot(test_eval.index, test_eval["baseline_vol_ann"], label="Test baseline (ann)", linewidth=1, linestyle="--")
        plt.title(f"{ticker} test volatility forecast (annualized)")
        plt.ylabel("Annualized volatility")
        plt.legend()
        plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
        plt.tight_layout()
        plt.show()

    # return outputs
    return {
        "ticker": ticker,
        "freq": freq,
        "mean_model": mean_model,
        "vol_model": vol_model,
        "p": p,
        "o": o,
        "q": q,
        "dist": dist,
        "scale_returns": scale_returns,
        "realized_vol_window": realized_vol_window,
        "trading_days_per_year": trading_days_per_year,
        "trading_seconds_per_day": trading_seconds_per_day,
        "annualization_factor": annualization_factor,

        "ts_df": ts_df,
        "series_return": series_return,
        "series_session": series_session,

        "train_ret": train_ret,
        "val_ret": val_ret,
        "test_ret": test_ret,
        "train_val_ret": train_val_ret,

        "results_train": results_train,
        "results_train_val": results_train_val,

        "std_resid_train": std_resid,
        "cond_vol_train": cond_vol_train_scaled / scale_returns,
        "cond_vol_train_ann": (cond_vol_train_scaled / scale_returns) * annualization_factor,
        "ljung_box_std_resid": lb_resid,
        "ljung_box_std_resid_sq": lb_sq,

        "val_vol_forecast": val_vol_forecast,
        "val_vol_forecast_ann": val_vol_forecast_ann,
        "val_realized_vol": val_realized_vol,
        "val_realized_vol_ann": val_realized_vol_ann,

        "val_baseline_mae_vol": val_baseline_mae_vol,
        "val_baseline_rmse_vol": val_baseline_rmse_vol,
        "val_baseline_mse_vol": val_baseline_mse_vol,
        "val_baseline_qlike": val_baseline_qlike,
        "val_baseline_mae_vol_ann": val_baseline_mae_vol_ann,
        "val_baseline_rmse_vol_ann": val_baseline_rmse_vol_ann,
        "val_baseline_mse_vol_ann": val_baseline_mse_vol_ann,
        "val_baseline_qlike_ann": val_baseline_qlike_ann,

        "test_vol_forecast": test_vol_forecast,
        "test_vol_forecast_ann": test_vol_forecast_ann,
        "test_realized_vol": test_realized_vol,
        "test_realized_vol_ann": test_realized_vol_ann,
        "test_baseline_vol": test_baseline_vol,
        "test_baseline_vol_ann": test_baseline_vol_ann,
        "test_eval": test_eval,
        "test_mae_vol": test_mae_vol,
        "test_rmse_vol": test_rmse_vol,
        "test_mse_vol": test_mse_vol,

ARIMA and GARCH Model Conclusions¶

1. Mean dynamics are weak¶

Across tickers, ARIMA constants are essentially zero. That means there is very little persistent drift in the 10-second returns.

2. ARIMA does detect short-run dependence¶

Most tickers show nonzero AR(1) and MA(1) coefficients, so there is some short-run return structure. But that structure is limited and does not fully describe the series.

3. Residuals remain problematic under ARIMA¶

Even after ARIMA fitting:

  • residual autocorrelation often remains at higher lags,

  • residuals are strongly non-normal,

  • kurtosis is extremely high,

  • heteroskedasticity remains significant.

ARIMA is not the main model to use then.

4. Volatility persistence is the dominant feature¶

Under GARCH, alpha and beta are positive across tickers, and several contracts have especially high beta, indicating persistent volatility clustering.

5. Heavy tails are consistent across contracts¶

The Student’s t degrees-of-freedom parameter is usually around 4, which is strong evidence that shocks are heavy-tailed rather than Gaussian.

6. GARCH improves out-of-sample volatility forecasts¶

For the visible tickers, GARCH consistently beats the constant-volatility baseline in validation and test MAE/RMSE/QLIKE. That is one of the strongest practical findings in the PDFs.

Model comparisons¶

ARIMA relatively stronger¶

Among the visible results, ARIMA looks relatively less weak for:

  • ZT.c.0

  • ZF.c.0

  • TN.c.0

These still have the same diagnostic problems, but their forecast errors are comparatively smaller.

ARIMA relatively weaker¶

ARIMA looks weakest for:

  • CL.c.0

  • BZ.c.0

  • ZN.c.0

  • UB.c.0

These show stronger evidence that volatility dominates the dynamics or that residual issues remain severe.

GARCH relatively stronger¶

The strongest visible GARCH cases are:

  • BZ.c.0

  • ZT.c.0

  • UB.c.0

  • ZB.c.0

These combine strong persistence with clearly better out-of-sample volatility forecasting.

GARCH relatively weaker¶

These are still good GARCH cases, but a bit less clean than the strongest ones:

  • TN.c.0

  • ZF.c.0

  • CL.c.0

The main reason is that standardized residual autocorrelation is still present even though squared residual diagnostics are strong.

Final comparison¶

Overall, comparing across tickers:

  • ARIMA captures a small amount of short-run dependence in returns, but it leaves major distributional and volatility problems unresolved.

  • GARCH matches the structure of the data better because it models persistent, heavy-tailed, time-varying volatility directly.

The main empirical pattern across the tickers is that these high-frequency return series are driven much more by volatility clustering and heavy tails than by predictable mean dynamics. Because of that, GARCH is the better model overall, while ARIMA is best treated as a limited mean specification rather than the main framework.

Potential Real-World Application:¶

Compare to UX1 Data - Trading Strategy to try to make money based off forecasted volatility by trading VIX futures. (UX1 data from Bloomberg)

The strategy builds an hourly cross-asset volatility signal from the eight non-UX1 futures, which are sampled at 10-second frequency. Since UX1 is 1-minute data, one UX1 hour corresponds to 60 UX1 rows and 360 rows in the 10-second futures data. For each 360-row futures block, the code takes the latest annualized realized volatility and the latest annualized GARCH forecast volatility for each ticker, averages those across the eight contracts, and generates a signal: go long UX1 if average realized volatility is below average projected volatility, otherwise go short.

That signal is then shifted forward by one block to avoid lookahead bias and mapped onto UX1, where positions are updated every 60 UX1 rows. PnL is computed from changes in the UX1 price using a VIX futures multiplier of 1000 and a fixed starting position size. The output includes cumulative PnL, daily PnL, drawdown, and position plots so the strategy can be evaluated both at the intraday block level and in daily summary form.

As one example, in the sample period (Dec. 10, 2025 to Jan. 18, 2026), the strategy produces positive cumulative PnL before transaction costs.

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def build_intraday_signal_every_360(
    multi_out,
    signal_tickers,
    split="test",
    start_date="2025-11-18",
    end_date="2026-01-20",
    futures_block_rows=360
):
    results = multi_out["results"]
    frames = []

    start_date = pd.to_datetime(start_date)
    end_date = pd.to_datetime(end_date)

    for ticker in signal_tickers:
        eval_df = results[ticker][f"{split}_eval"][["realized_vol_ann", "forecast_vol_ann"]].copy()
        eval_df = eval_df.sort_index().copy()
        eval_df = eval_df.loc[
            (eval_df.index >= start_date) &
            (eval_df.index <= end_date)
        ].copy()

        eval_df = eval_df.reset_index()
        time_col = eval_df.columns[0]
        eval_df = eval_df.rename(columns={time_col: "ts"})

        eval_df["fut_row"] = np.arange(len(eval_df))
        eval_df["block"] = eval_df["fut_row"] // futures_block_rows

        block_df = (
            eval_df.groupby("block", as_index=False)
            .agg(
                ts=("ts", "last"),
                realized_vol_ann=("realized_vol_ann", "last"),
                forecast_vol_ann=("forecast_vol_ann", "last")
            )
            .rename(columns={
                "realized_vol_ann": f"{ticker}_realized",
                "forecast_vol_ann": f"{ticker}_projected"
            })
        )

        frames.append(block_df)

    if not frames:
        raise ValueError("No usable futures signal data found")

    panel = frames[0][["block", "ts", f"{signal_tickers[0]}_realized", f"{signal_tickers[0]}_projected"]].copy()

    for i, ticker in enumerate(signal_tickers[1:], start=1):
        tmp = frames[i][["block", f"{ticker}_realized", f"{ticker}_projected"]].copy()
        panel = panel.merge(tmp, on="block", how="inner")

    panel = panel.sort_values("block").reset_index(drop=True)

    realized_cols = [f"{t}_realized" for t in signal_tickers]
    projected_cols = [f"{t}_projected" for t in signal_tickers]

    panel["avg_realized"] = panel[realized_cols].mean(axis=1)
    panel["avg_projected"] = panel[projected_cols].mean(axis=1)
    panel["signal"] = np.where(panel["avg_realized"] < panel["avg_projected"], 1, -1)

    return panel


def build_ux1_strategy_hourly_aligned(
    multi_out,
    ux1_csv_path,
    signal_tickers,
    split="test",
    start_date="2025-11-18",
    end_date="2026-01-20",
    qty=1,
    multiplier=1000.0,
    ux1_block_rows=60,
    futures_block_rows=360
):
    signal_panel = build_intraday_signal_every_360(
        multi_out=multi_out,
        signal_tickers=signal_tickers,
        split=split,
        start_date=start_date,
        end_date=end_date,
        futures_block_rows=futures_block_rows
    )

    signal_panel["position_block"] = signal_panel["signal"].shift(1).fillna(0)

    ux1 = pd.read_csv(ux1_csv_path)
    ux1["Date"] = pd.to_datetime(ux1["Date"])
    ux1 = ux1.rename(columns={"UX1 Index Price": "ux1_price"}).copy()
    ux1 = ux1[
        (ux1["Date"] >= pd.to_datetime(start_date)) &
        (ux1["Date"] <= pd.to_datetime(end_date))
    ].copy()

    ux1 = ux1.sort_values("Date").reset_index(drop=True)
    ux1["ux1_row"] = np.arange(len(ux1))
    ux1["block"] = ux1["ux1_row"] // ux1_block_rows

    ux1 = ux1.merge(
        signal_panel[["block", "ts", "avg_realized", "avg_projected", "signal", "position_block"]],
        on="block",
        how="left"
    )

    ux1["position"] = qty * ux1["position_block"].ffill().fillna(0)
    ux1["price_change"] = ux1["ux1_price"].diff().fillna(0)
    ux1["pnl"] = ux1["position"].shift(1).fillna(0) * ux1["price_change"] * multiplier
    ux1["cum_pnl"] = ux1["pnl"].cumsum()
    ux1["running_peak"] = ux1["cum_pnl"].cummax()
    ux1["drawdown"] = ux1["cum_pnl"] - ux1["running_peak"]

    return ux1, signal_panel


def make_daily_summary(ux1_strat):
    daily = ux1_strat.groupby("Date", as_index=False).agg(
        daily_pnl=("pnl", "sum"),
        end_position=("position", "last"),
        avg_realized=("avg_realized", "last"),
        avg_projected=("avg_projected", "last"),
        cum_pnl=("cum_pnl", "last")
    )
    daily["running_peak"] = daily["cum_pnl"].cummax()
    daily["drawdown"] = daily["cum_pnl"] - daily["running_peak"]
    return daily

Key Takeaways¶

ARIMA finds weak short-run dependence in every ticker (significant AR and MA terms), but the practical forecasting value is essentially zero — predictions flatline to the mean within a few steps. The real finding from ARIMA is in the diagnostics: massive kurtosis, heteroskedasticity, and residual autocorrelation that all point toward needing a volatility model.

GARCH captures volatility clustering well. Squared standardised residuals pass Ljung-Box for every ticker (p ≈ 1.0), confirming the variance model is adequate. GARCH beats the constant-volatility baseline for all 9 tickers, with improvements from 9% (6E) to 39% (CL). Energy contracts benefit most; low-volatility contracts like ZT and 6E benefit least.

Heavy tails persist everywhere. Student-t ν ≈ 4.0 across the board — even after GARCH filtering, extreme returns are far more frequent than Gaussian.

Convergence is a concern. All GARCH fits produced optimizer warnings. Parameters at round values (α = 0.05, β = 0.93) suggest boundary constraints may be binding rather than true optima.

Applications¶

  • Include a potential trading strategy where if predicted volatility is greater than trailing realized volatility, then buy UX1 Vix future. Otherwise, short it.

Limitations¶

  • Realized volatility proxy is noisy (6-obs / 60-second window)

  • Overnight session gaps are not explicitly modelled

  • Same GARCH(1,1) + Student-t specification applied to all tickers

  • All optimizers failed to fully converge

What Could Come Next¶

  • GJR-GARCH / EGARCH for leverage effects

  • ARMA-GARCH to jointly model mean + variance (especially for ZN, ZF)

  • HAR-RV as an alternative volatility framework

  • Intraday seasonality correction (open/close U-shape)
